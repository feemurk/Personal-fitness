Phase 01 · 10–12 min
Breathing & core pressure
Start here every time, including the 10-min version
✓
Supine TVA Breathing
3 sets · 5–6 breaths · band not needed
▾
Hockey Golf
How to do it

Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor. Let everything go. Breathe in slowly through your nose and try to send the air sideways and backward — into your obliques and the sides of your lower ribcage — rather than lifting your chest. The ribs stay down. Breathe out slowly, and as you do, let the lower belly draw very gently inward. Nothing forceful. Just a subtle gathering.

What you're looking for: a sense of the trunk filling 360 degrees, like a cylinder pressurizing from the inside. The floor contact at your lower back stays consistent throughout.
The witness cue

This is a good exercise for that dissociative awareness you described. Let the breath happen while you watch it from the outside — noticing what moves, what doesn't, where the expansion feels blocked. You're observing your own body re-learning a pattern it lost at the desk.

Why it matters for golf

Anterior pelvic tilt — your desk posture — forces the spine into extension at address. That extension restricts how far your thorax can rotate. As a left-hander with a natural fade, your shoulder turn is already working against a tightened backswing. This breath restores the pelvis-rib relationship that unlocks that rotation.

Why it matters for hockey

Core pressure — intra-abdominal pressure built by this breathing pattern — is what transfers force from skate to ice without the spine compensating. Skating without it is like pushing through a soft wall. Every stride you take right now is slightly inefficient because of this. This exercise is where you rebuild the wall.

✓
Deadbug — Leg Extension
3 sets · 6 reps per side · band not needed
▾
Hockey Golf Knee Safe
How to do it

Stay on your back from the breathing. Keep the TVA engagement from the last exercise — that subtle gathering feeling. Slowly lower one heel toward the floor while the other leg stays bent. The lower back must not lose contact with the floor. If it does, your range was too much. Return and switch sides. Move slowly enough that you can feel what's happening.

The job of the core here is to do nothing. The leg moves; the trunk stays absolutely still. That stillness under load is the skill you're building.
The witness cue

Watch yourself doing this from the outside — notice which side the back loses contact first. Notice if the ribs flare upward when the leg descends. These are data points, not failures. You're mapping your own compensation patterns in real time.

Golf crossover

The anti-rotation control you're building here is the same demand your pelvis faces during your downswing. For a left-hander, the lead hip must resist rotation while the upper body delivers the club. This exercise trains that exact timing — just with a leg instead of a club.

Right knee: Full leg extension is low-load here. If you feel anything anterior at the knee, reduce how far the leg travels — don't stop, just shorten the range.
Phase 02 · 10 min
Scapular & serratus stability
Connects your rib cage to your shoulder — the missing link in your swing and your skating posture
✓
Wall Y Raises
3 sets · 10 reps · band optional for resistance
▾
Hockey Golf
How to do it

Stand with your head and back lightly touching a wall. Raise your arms into a Y shape — thumbs pointing away from the wall. Slide them upward slowly. Keep the ribs down, the lower back from arching away from the wall, and the shoulders away from your ears. The wall gives you instant honest feedback on whether you're compensating.

You're looking for the lower part of the shoulder blade to engage — a pulling sensation underneath and inward, not a shrug. If the shoulders ride up, you've handed the work to the wrong muscles. Reset.
Golf — why this matters specifically for you

As a left-hander, your lead shoulder (right) needs to move under your chin during the backswing. Inhibited serratus — which is almost certainly part of your postural picture — restricts this. Your body compensates by lifting the shoulder instead of rotating it, which is one mechanical source of the over-the-top tendency that produces your fade. This exercise begins to restore that movement.

✓
Plank with Serratus Push
3 sets · 30 seconds · band not needed
▾
Hockey
How to do it

High plank on hands, or forearm plank — whichever keeps your lower back neutral. Hold the plank with a long spine. Every 5–6 seconds, push the floor away and spread the shoulder blades apart — making the upper back wide and flat — then return to the resting plank. The rest of the hold is stillness.

The push-away feeling: shoulder blades wrapping around the rib cage outward, like wings opening slightly. The neck stays long and uninvolved throughout.
Hockey payoff

Torso stiffness under load is the platform your skating strides push from. Without it, force generated by the hips bleeds into spinal movement rather than transferring to the blade. This drill builds the bridge between your breathing work and your leg power.

✓
Serratus Wall Press Hold
3 sets · 20 seconds · band not needed
▾
Golf
How to do it

Face a wall. Both hands at shoulder height, arms straight. Press actively into the wall and drive your shoulder blades forward and outward around your rib cage. Hold. Neck relaxed, ribs down, jaw unclenched. Simply press and stay.

Golf payoff

Your natural fade as a left-hander has a postural component. When the shoulder blades wing off the ribcage — which is part of your desk posture picture — your shoulder setup at address tilts in a way that promotes an outward swing path. Rebuilding serratus strength helps your blades sit flush, which improves your address geometry and gradually softens the fade without you consciously manipulating the swing.

Phase 03 · 15–20 min
Lower body strength & knee alignment
Rebuilding what 14 months of tendinopathy took — systematically and safely
✓
Split Squat — Step & Band Assist
3 sets · 6–8 reps per leg · step + band optional
▾
Hockey Golf Knee Rehab
How to do it

Rear foot elevated on your step or a stair. Front foot forward. Stand tall, torso upright. Lower slowly — 3 counts — letting the front knee travel forward over the toes without collapsing inward. Push through the full front foot to return. Start with bodyweight. Add your 10lb kettlebell in one or both hands when it feels controlled rather than effortful.

Band use: Loop a band around your front knee and anchor it to something beside you. This gives the knee something to push outward against, which activates the glute medius automatically. Particularly useful for the right leg in early weeks.
The witness cue

Watch the front knee track — ideally in a mirror or glass door. Notice whether it drifts inward as fatigue sets in. That drift is your glute medius stepping out. It's not a fault; it's feedback. Push the knee out, feel the hip engage, note the difference. This is the proprioceptive loop that rebuilds the pattern.

Hockey payoff

Every skating stride is this movement — one leg pushing, one leg loaded. The right leg asymmetry you feel on the ice traces directly to the strength deficit this exercise addresses. As both legs equalize here, stride symmetry returns. The step you've lost isn't gone; it's waiting for this work to finish.

Golf payoff

Your left (lead) leg must create a stable post through impact. Single-leg strength here prevents your left knee from riding outward, which robs you of speed before it reaches the club. This is one of the cleanest distance gains available to you without changing your swing at all.

Right knee at 2–3/10: You're cleared to load progressively. Slow, controlled descent protects the tendon. If pain climbs above 3/10 during a set, reduce depth — don't push through a rising signal. Log the pain score after each session.
✓
Spanish Squats — Band
3 sets · 10 reps · loop band around fixed point
▾
Tendon Rehab Hockey
How to do it

Loop a resistance band around a fixed point at knee height — a table leg, door handle, stair rail. Step back so the band sits behind both knees under tension. Stand feet hip width. Sit back and down into a squat, keeping your torso upright and shins relatively vertical — the band holds the knees in place while the quads load. Hold 2 seconds at the bottom. Return slowly.

This is tendon rehabilitation in the form of a strength exercise. The slow loading and the brief hold at the bottom is exactly how patellar tendons are stimulated to remodel after tendinopathy. This isn't supplementary — it's medicine.
Hockey payoff

The skating crouch demands sustained quad loading at a knee angle very close to this. You're conditioning the tendon for the exact position skating puts you in — building the tolerance back up without the reactive load of ice.

✓
Lateral Step Downs
3 sets · 8 reps per leg · step required
▾
Hockey Knee Alignment
How to do it

Stand on your step on one foot. The other foot hangs off the side. Slowly lower that free foot toward the floor — 3 counts — without touching down. Return. Use fingertips on a wall for balance only, not support. Watch the standing knee in a mirror or reflective surface.

The witness cue

This exercise was designed for exactly the kind of self-observation you described. Watch the knee — does it drift inward? At what point in the descent? Does it happen earlier on the right leg than the left? That's information. Push the knee outward against the drift, feel which hip muscle fires to hold it. You are teaching your nervous system a new default.

The knee tracking outward over the middle two toes: that's the target. Achieving it means the glute medius fired correctly. When it starts happening without you thinking about it — that's when the change has become neurological, not just muscular.
Phase 04 · 10 min
Hip control & lateral power
Turning strength into the force patterns both sports require
✓
Driver Stance Lateral Push
3 sets · 10 reps per side · band anchored low
▾
Hockey Golf
How to do it

Take your driver address position — wide base, hip hinge, spine angle forward. Anchor a band low to something fixed and loop it around one ankle for light resistance. From this position, push your foot outward into the floor while simultaneously driving that knee outward. Hold 2 seconds at the peak push. Feel the chain: foot arch, ankle, knee, glute — all working together in one lateral push. Switch sides.

This is the highest-efficiency exercise in your program. The same kinetic chain fires in a skating push and in your golf swing load. You are training both sports at once, in a position that already feels like sports.
Golf — specific to your swing

For a left-hander, the right side loads the backswing and the left side drives the downswing. This drill builds both halves. The right side push trains the load into your trail hip. The left side push trains the stable post your lead leg needs through impact. This is where your distance ceiling gets raised — not by swinging harder, but by building the platform the swing fires from.

✓
Banded Monster Walks
3 sets · 10 steps each direction · band around knees
▾
Hockey Knee Rehab
How to do it

Band around your knees or ankles. Drop into a slight hip hinge — the hockey stance position. Step sideways slowly, maintaining constant outward pressure against the band. Knees push out against it the entire time. Ten steps one direction, then return. Do not let the knees cave inward between steps.

The witness cue

As you step, notice whether the glute fatigue comes first or the quad. In the early weeks it will be the quads — they're compensating. As the glute medius strengthens over weeks, you'll feel the burn move to the outer hip instead. That shift is the signal that your body is reassigning the job to the right muscle.

Hockey payoff

Glute medius weakness is the root cause of your bilateral knee valgus on ice. This is the most direct fix available. Over 6–8 weeks you'll notice the knees staying outward on the ice without conscious effort. That's the nervous system update completing.

Lateral Skater Bounds
3 sets · 8 reps per side · weeks 7+ for right leg
▾
Hockey
How to do it

Push off one leg laterally, land on the opposite leg and hold the landing — 2 to 3 seconds — before the next bound. Soft landing with a bent knee. Distance is secondary to landing quality. Build the distance over weeks, not sessions.

The witness cue

On the landing: observe the knee. Does it track outward? Does the hip drop? Is the landing quiet or heavy? Every landing is a diagnostic. You're watching your own body in slow motion, using each rep to update the motor program.

Right leg bounds: only add these when your right-leg split squat load matches your left within 10%. That's the clearance gate. Until then, monster walks and step-downs carry the lateral training.
✓
Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift
3 sets · 8 per leg · 10lb KB or bodyweight
▾
Hockey Golf
How to do it

Stand on one leg. Hold your 10lb kettlebell in the opposite hand. Hinge at the hip — not the waist — while extending the free leg behind you. Keep the spine long, hips level, standing knee soft. Return by driving the hip forward. Slow enough to feel the posterior chain working throughout. Bodyweight is fine if the KB feels like too much.

The witness cue

This is a balance and proprioception exercise as much as a strength one. Find a fixed point to look at. Notice the micro-corrections happening in your foot — all those small adjustments are your nervous system learning. The wobble is not a failure; it's the work.

Hockey payoff

The glide phase — balanced on one blade after a push — is this exact demand. Every second you can hold a clean single-leg balance in training translates directly to glide time and confidence on the ice. This is where skating steadiness is built, not on the ice.

Golf payoff

Maintaining spine angle through impact — not standing up early — is a hip hinge problem. This drill rebuilds the pattern and the strength. As it improves, you'll find it easier to stay down through the ball, which directly affects strike quality and consistency with your 7-iron.

Your fade, your posture, and the hidden link

A natural fade as a left-hander is common — but yours has a postural amplifier. Anterior pelvic tilt pushes your lumbar spine into extension at address. That extension tightens the thoracolumbar fascia and compresses the rotation available in your thoracic spine. Less thoracic rotation means a shorter backswing coil, which your body compensates for by swinging the arms on a more outward path — the classic producer of a fade or pull.

As the TVA breathing work and hip mobility in this program begins to restore pelvic neutral, you'll feel your address posture settle — spine taller, hips less pitched forward. The backswing will deepen. The fade will soften, not because you've changed your swing consciously, but because your body can finally do what you're asking it to do.

The one breath before every shot

Before address, take your TVA breath. In through the nose, feel the sides and back of the ribs expand, exhale and feel the pelvis settle. One breath. That's it. It sets core pressure, neutralizes the pelvis, and gives you a consistent physical starting point for every swing. Tour players do this without naming it. Now you have the name.

Scapular freedom & your shoulder turn

Your lead shoulder (right, as a left-hander) needs to move under your chin during the backswing. When the serratus is inhibited — which is your current situation from desk posture and rib flare — the scapula wings off the ribcage. That winging restricts the path the shoulder can travel, so you lift it instead of rotating it. The lift shallows the shoulder turn and promotes the over-the-top move.

The Wall Y Raises and Serratus Wall Press are directly rebuilding this. By week 6, you should feel the right shoulder move more deeply under your chin without any conscious swing thought. That's the fix arriving.

Distance — the real math

You don't need to swing harder. You need to stop losing the speed you already generate. Lead-leg collapse, early extension, poor hip clearance — each one bleeds speed before it reaches the face. This program addresses all three simultaneously.

Three drills to add at the range

GLUTE ACTIVATION — PRE-ROUND

10 monster walk steps in driver stance before first swing. Wakes the glute medius up after sitting. Takes 90 seconds. Makes an immediate difference in how stable your first few swings feel.

SPLIT STANCE SWING — 7-IRON

Pull your lead foot (left) back into a staggered stance. Hit half-swing shots. Forces you to feel and use the trail hip properly — loading it rather than sliding past it. 10–15 balls, then return to normal stance and notice what's changed in the feel.

STEP-THROUGH DRILL — HIP CLEARANCE

After impact, let your trail foot (right) step forward as the follow-through completes. Trains lead hip clearance — the rotation of the left hip out of the way — which is limited by the same anterior tilt that tightens your skating stride. Start with short irons, then bring it to the mini driver.

Golf and hockey share a kinetic chain.
Every exercise in this program is building both at once.

These drills go at the start of each skate, when you're fresh. Quality of movement matters more than reps. When fatigue sets in, stop the drill — don't grind through bad patterns.

Glide-Push Distance Drill

Push once from one leg with full hip extension — drive all the way through until the pushing leg is straight behind you — then glide as far as possible on the opposite blade before the next push. Maximum distance per stride, not maximum strides.

Feel: the push coming through the middle of the blade and finishing at the toe. The glide leg stays bent, balanced, quiet. That single-blade balance is where your SL-RDL work shows up. Time yourself on one blade in each direction.

5–6 pushes per side. When your right-leg glide time equals your left — that's the stride symmetry restored. That's when speed comes back naturally.

Edge Pressure Walk

Skate slowly down the ice and on each push, consciously press through the inside edge — feel the arch of the foot engaging, the ankle loading, the knee tracking outward. Exaggerate the knee-out position. One full length of the ice before skating naturally. You're ingraining the same lateral push pattern you train with monster walks and step-downs off the ice.

One-Leg Glide Balance Test

Gain a little momentum, then lift one skate and balance on the other blade. Hold. Time it with your phone. Log it in the Progress tab. This is your clearest weekly indicator of how the lateral chain is recovering.

About the early fatigue

The fatigue you feel early in a skate right now is not a fitness problem. It's a compensation cost. When the glute medius is weak, the lower back, hip flexors, and quads overwork to stabilise what the glute should be handling. Those muscles are not designed for sustained load — they fatigue quickly.

As weeks 4–8 progress, the fatigue will push later into the skate. That's the signal — not improved cardio, but improved mechanical efficiency. The body stops spending energy on compensation and starts using it for skating.

Skate setup
🔪
Blade hollow: 5/8"

Improved glide reduces muscular cost per stride during recovery. Less quad load per shift while the tendon continues to heal.

⚖️
Edge level check

Ask your sharpener to verify both edges are balanced. An unlevel blade creates a subtle compensation pattern that adds stress to the knee on every stride — invisible until it isn't.

📐
Profiling: Quad 1 (optional, week 4+)

If skating still feels heel-biased after four weeks, Quad 1 profiling shifts the balance point forward, encouraging a more mid-blade stance that naturally reduces anterior knee load. Ask your sharpener.

👟
Lacing: second-from-top firm, top loose

Allows the ankle to flex properly into the skating crouch, which reduces compensatory quad overloading that aggravates the patellar tendon.
